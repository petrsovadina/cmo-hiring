> ## The CEE Advantage
>
> **Talent density at reasonable cost.** Brno has two major universities producing strong CS graduates. Our engineering cost is roughly 60% of Berlin and 30% of London -- but the quality is world-class. Several of our engineers have contributions to major open-source projects.
>
> **Proximity to customers.** Our portfolio companies serve SMBs across Europe. Being in the center of the continent, we're within a 2-hour flight of nearly every European market. The timezone works perfectly for EU business hours.
>
> **Regulatory pragmatism.** Czech Republic has a stable business environment, EU membership (so GDPR compliance is native, not bolted on), and a government that generally stays out of tech companies' way.
>
> ## What We'd Do Differently
>
> Not everything is rosy. Here's what we've learned the hard way:
>
> **Brand perception matters.** When you're not in a "known" tech hub, you work harder to attract attention from potential acquirees, partners, and investors. Our response: let the products speak. Reservio's 20M+ bookings are a better pitch than any location.
>
> **International hiring needs intentional effort.** Brno is great for Czech and Slovak talent, but attracting international candidates requires active employer branding. We've invested heavily in this over the past two years.
>
> **Ecosystem support is thinner.** There's no equivalent of Station F or TechStars locally. We've had to build our own support structures and connections.
>

>
> We often get asked: why a holding company instead of one big product?
>
> Simple: commerce is too fragmented for one tool. A salon owner booking clients (Reservio) has different needs than an e-shop optimizing checkout (Convertim). But they're connected -- they're all commerce businesses trying to serve customers better.
>
> Our model: acquire specialized tools, keep them independent, but build bridges between them. A Reservio customer who also runs an online store can use Smartsupp. A Shopsys merchant can plug in Tanganica for marketing automation.
>
> The compound effect is real. Each new company makes the ecosystem more valuable.
>
> ## Key Takeaways
>
> - Location matters less than you think for B2B SaaS
> - CEE offers a genuine talent and cost advantage that's narrowing but still significant
> - The holding model works when products share customers but serve different needs
> - GDPR compliance from day one is a feature, not a bug

Seed: Headless commerce and AI convergence

> **Title:** Headless Commerce + AI: What We're Building at Shopsys for Large E-Commerce
>
> **Meta:** How headless commerce architecture enables AI-powered personalization and automation at scale -- insights from Shopsys and the Abugo portfolio.
>
> *7 min read | July 15, 2026 | Abugo Team*
>
> ## The Monolith Problem
>
> Large e-commerce stores running monolithic platforms face a recurring frustration: every new feature requires touching the whole system. Want to add AI-powered product recommendations? That's a 3-month project because it's tangled with the checkout flow, inventory, and CMS.
>
> At Shopsys, our headless e-commerce platform, we built the architecture specifically to avoid this. The frontend is decoupled from the backend. Services communicate through APIs. Adding a new capability -- like an AI layer -- means connecting a new service, not rewriting existing ones.
>
> ## Why Headless Matters for AI
>
> AI thrives on clean data and flexible integration points. Headless architecture provides both:
>
> **Clean APIs = clean AI inputs.** When your product catalog, customer data, and order history are accessible through structured APIs, feeding them into an AI model is straightforward. No scraping your own database.
>
> **Modular services = composable AI.** You can add an AI recommendation engine alongside your existing search, personalization alongside your existing CMS, chatbot alongside your existing support -- without each one knowing about the others.
>
> **Flexible frontends = adaptive experiences.** When your frontend is decoupled, you can serve different AI-personalized experiences to different customer segments without backend changes.
>
> ## Real-World Examples from Our Portfolio
>
> Within the Abugo ecosystem, we're seeing early versions of this:
>
> - **Smartsupp + Shopsys:** AI chatbot (Mira) integrated into Shopsys storefronts via API. The chat widget has product catalog context, so it can answer "Do you have this in blue?" accurately.
> - **Tanganica + Shopsys:** Automated marketing campaigns that pull product data from Shopsys APIs. When inventory changes, campaigns adjust automatically.
> - **Convertim + Shopsys:** Checkout optimization that analyzes abandonment patterns and dynamically adjusts the checkout flow.
>
> None of these integrations required modifying Shopsys core. They're additional services connected through APIs.
>
> ## The Technical Reality
>
> Headless isn't magic. It comes with real costs:
>
> - **Higher initial complexity.** You need API design expertise from day one.
> - **DevOps overhead.** More services = more things to monitor and deploy.
> - **Frontend investment.** Without a built-in storefront, you're building (or buying) your own.
>
> The payoff: when you need to add AI capabilities 2 years in, it's weeks not months. When you need to swap your recommendation engine, it's an API change not a rewrite.
>
> ## Key Takeaways
>
> - Headless architecture is an AI readiness strategy, not just a frontend trend
> - The real value is composability: add new AI services without touching existing ones
> - Cross-product integration (like Smartsupp chat on Shopsys stores) becomes trivial with clean APIs
> - The upfront cost is real -- headless makes sense for large stores, not every Shopify merchant
>
> ## What's Next
>
> We're investing in making these cross-product integrations easier and more powerful. The vision: a commerce store that automatically uses the best AI tools from across the Abugo portfolio, connected through standard APIs.
>

**Blog [Pillar 4] -- 08:00 CET**
Seed: What 20M bookings reveal about SMB behavior

> **Title:** What 20 Million Bookings Taught Us About Small Business Behavior
>
> **Meta:** Analysis of booking patterns from Reservio's 20M+ annual bookings reveals surprising insights about SMB operations, no-shows, and customer behavior.
>
> *8 min read | July 22, 2026 | Abugo Team*
>
> ## The Dataset Nobody Talks About
>
> When people think about "big data" in commerce, they think Amazon, Shopify, or Stripe. But there's a massive, underexplored dataset sitting in the appointment economy: the millions of bookings made daily at salons, clinics, gyms, and repair shops.
>
> At Reservio, we process over 20 million bookings per year. Each one tells a story about how small businesses and their customers interact. We've spent the past year analyzing these patterns, and the insights are surprisingly universal.
>
> ## Finding 1: The Monday Paradox
>
> Mondays have the highest booking demand but the lowest business availability. Many service businesses close Mondays (legacy from traditional rest days), creating artificial scarcity at the exact moment customers are most ready to book.
>
> Businesses that opened Monday slots saw a 12% average revenue increase with no additional marketing spend.
>
> ## Finding 2: The Rescheduling Effect
>
> We expected that offering easy rescheduling would increase cancellations. The opposite happened. When customers can reschedule with one tap (instead of calling to cancel), the overall no-show rate drops significantly.
>
> The psychology: calling to cancel feels confrontational. Tapping "reschedule" feels helpful. Lower friction = better behavior.
>
> ## Finding 3: The Review Window
>
> The optimal time to request a review is 2-4 hours after the appointment ends. Not immediately (too eager), not the next day (they've moved on). Within this window, review completion rates are highest.
>
> This matters because for most local service businesses, Google Reviews are the #1 acquisition channel. More reviews = more visibility = more bookings. It's a compound loop.
>
> ## Finding 4: Seasonal Patterns Are More Predictable Than You'd Think
>
> Beauty and wellness bookings peak in the 2 weeks before major holidays, drop during holidays, and surge in January. Fitness bookings spike in January and September. Dental follows a school-year pattern.
>
> Businesses that adjust their staffing and availability to these patterns (which we can now automate) see measurable revenue improvements.
>
> ## Finding 5: The SMB Technology Adoption Curve
>
> When we look at how business owners adopt features, there's a clear pattern: they start with online booking (the core pain), then add reminders (the obvious win), then reviews (the growth lever), and finally analytics (the optimization phase).
>
> Each phase takes about 3-6 months. Trying to sell all four upfront overwhelms them. Progressive feature discovery, timed to their readiness, works best.
>
> ## Key Takeaways
>
> - The appointment economy is data-rich but under-analyzed
> - Small behavior changes (open Mondays, easy rescheduling) drive outsized results
> - Timing matters enormously for review collection and customer communication
> - Progressive feature adoption beats feature overload for SMBs
>

**Blog [Pillar 2] -- 08:00 CET**
Seed: SaaS acquisition playbook from European perspective

> **Title:** The European SaaS Acquisition Playbook: What We Learned Buying 7 Companies
>
> **Meta:** Abugo shares practical lessons from acquiring and integrating 7 commerce SaaS companies in Central Europe. Real numbers, real challenges.
>
> *9 min read | July 29, 2026 | Abugo Team*
>
> ## Why We Buy (and What We Don't)
>
> Abugo exists because we believe the commerce stack is too fragmented for any single product to serve well, but too connected for complete independence. A salon owner who books appointments also needs payment processing, customer feedback, and marketing. An e-shop that sells online also needs chat support, checkout optimization, and ad automation.
>
> Our thesis: acquire specialized, product-led SaaS companies that serve commerce businesses. Keep them independent. Build bridges between them.
>
> Over the past several years, this thesis has materialized into 7 portfolio companies and 150 people. Here's what we've learned.
>
> ## Lesson 1: Product Quality Is Non-Negotiable
>
> We've passed on numerous acquisition targets that had great revenue but mediocre products. In SaaS, product quality compounds. A great product retains customers, generates word-of-mouth, and attracts talent. A mediocre product needs constant marketing spend just to maintain revenue.
>
> Our filter: would we use this product ourselves? Can we talk to 5 customers who genuinely love it? If not, we walk.
>
> ## Lesson 2: Cultural Fit > Financial Metrics
>
> The most dangerous acquisition is one with perfect metrics and toxic culture. We learned this by watching other holding companies struggle with integration. Now our process includes:
>
> - Meeting the full team, not just founders
> - Understanding how decisions are made (top-down vs. distributed)
> - Checking if the team ships regularly (sign of healthy engineering culture)
> - Assessing founder motivations (building vs. cashing out)
>
> ## Lesson 3: Don't Touch the Product (At First)
>
> The biggest temptation post-acquisition is to "optimize" the product. Resist it. The product was built by people who understand the market. Your job in the first 6 months is to listen, support, and remove obstacles -- not redesign.
>
> After 6 months, you've earned enough context to suggest changes. Before that, you're guessing with someone else's product.
>
> ## Lesson 4: Centralize Infrastructure, Not Strategy
>
> What we centralize: hosting, compliance, HR, finance, security.
> What we don't: product roadmap, go-to-market strategy, pricing, hiring decisions.
>
> The holding provides the foundation. Each company builds its own house.
>
> ## Lesson 5: European M&A Is Different
>
> Valuations are generally more reasonable in CEE than US/Western Europe. But the transaction complexity can be higher:
>
> - Multiple legal frameworks across EU countries
> - Different employment law per jurisdiction
> - Currency considerations (CZK, EUR, PLN)
> - Due diligence norms vary significantly by country
>
> Having local legal expertise isn't optional. It's essential.
>
> ## Key Takeaways
>
> - Acquire for product quality first, revenue second
> - Cultural due diligence prevents the hardest integration problems
> - Don't change what you just bought -- earn the right to have opinions
> - Centralize the boring stuff, decentralize the creative stuff
> - European SaaS M&A has unique dynamics -- local expertise is mandatory
>
